GrowTogether

A plant-care companion app concept, designed to help people track, water, and identify the plants in their home — built from the ground up through user flows, competing wireframe directions, and real usability testing.

Role

UX/UI Designer

Timeline

Winter 2026

Responsibilities

Information architecture, user flows, wireframing, usability testing, visual interface design

Selected Applications

Mobile App Concept Wireframing Usability Testing UI Design

The Problem

Keeping houseplants alive means juggling information scattered across memory, plant tags, and guesswork. GrowTogether's brief was to design a single place for the needs that came up again and again:

01

Organizing plant information in one place

02

Tracking ongoing care over time

03

Adding new plants to a personal collection

04

Identifying an unfamiliar plant on sight

05

Receiving timely, useful care reminders

06

Getting diagnosis support when something's wrong

Process

Research → Define → Explore → Test → Refine

Information Architecture & User Flow

The core task — adding a new plant and setting up its care routine — was mapped end to end before any screen was drawn: Launch → Discover → Virtual Garden → Search/Select/Scan → Plant Information Setup → Confirmation → back to Virtual Garden.

Exploring Two Approaches

One fast, single-scroll form. One guided, three-step wizard. Two honest bets on how people want to add a plant.

Exploring Two Approaches

The "Add a Plant" flow was designed and tested as two deliberately different interaction models, not a right-answer-and-a-throwaway. The design question: does a single, faster-scrolling form serve people better than a slower, more guided sequence?

Version A

A faster, single-scroll experience — every field on one screen

Version B

A more guided, three-step wizard — Plant Details, Environment, Watering Routine

Usability Testing

Both versions were tested with three participants completing the same task: search for and add an Aloe Vera to their garden. All three ultimately preferred Version A — but not for identical reasons, and not without acknowledging what Version B did well.

Participant 1

Found Version B's repeated confirmation steps frustrating — configuring care, then reviewing again, felt repetitive. Preferred Version A as faster and less tedious, despite Version A asking for more at once.

Participant 2

Completed both versions with zero navigation friction, but found Version B's step transitions abrupt. Preferred seeing all information "at once" over having it split across separate steps.

Participant 3

Praised Version B's step-by-step layout for breaking the task into smaller, "easier to take in" chunks — and suggested it would be more accessible for elderly users or beginners. Still preferred Version A overall, for speed.

The result was a trade-off, not a simple win. Speed and directness won out for this task and this group, but Version B's guided structure surfaced a real accessibility advantage worth carrying into future work rather than discarding.

Insight to Revision

Earlier working drafts of the Add-a-Plant flow used placeholder plant names ("Emma," "Mr. Ficus") that were later replaced in the finished screens ("Ollie," "Ms. Fig") as the flow was refined toward its final state. Not every visual change traces directly back to a usability finding — some are ordinary iteration.

Observation

Early drafts used placeholder naming and a rougher wireframe fidelity.

Design consideration

The finished flow needed names and details that felt like a real garden, not a template.

Reflection

Provisional — pending Drew's review

Version A's visual economy won out with this group — seeing every field at once, even at the cost of a longer single screen, read as faster and less tedious than moving through separate steps.

That result doesn't make Version B's guided structure a mistake. One participant's accessibility observation — that breaking the task into smaller steps was easier to take in — is a legitimate signal, not a consolation prize.

Testing more than one interaction model, rather than committing early to a single direction, was what surfaced that trade-off in the first place. A broader participant group — including people who identify as beginners or who have accessibility needs — would be the natural next test, to see whether Version B's advantage holds at scale or whether Version A's speed still wins out.
